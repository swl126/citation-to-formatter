# MLA 9th Edition

Source: [Pitt Library — MLA](https://pitt.libguides.com/citationhelp/mla9thedition)

MLA (Modern Language Association) style is used mainly in the humanities, especially language and literature. It features brief parenthetical in-text citations keyed to an alphabetical Works Cited list.

## Core elements & containers
Each Works Cited entry is built from the MLA "core elements," assembled in a fixed order (author, title of source, title of container, other contributors, version, number, publisher, publication date, location). A **container** is the larger whole that holds a source — e.g., an anthology containing a short story, or a website containing a webpage. Sources that are themselves inside a container that is inside another container (e.g., a journal article on a database) get two container tiers.

## What's new in the 9th edition
Same in-text and Works Cited format as the 8th edition. Adds a clarification for citing a film/DVD — generally list the director as a key contributor: `Blade Runner. 1982. Directed by Ridley Scott, director's cut, Warner Bros., 1992.`

## Author formatting (Works Cited)
`Last, First` for the first author only; additional authors are `First Last`. 2 authors: `Last, First, and First Last.` 3+ authors: first author + `et al.`

## In-text citation examples (verbatim from Pitt guide)

| Material Type | In-text Citation |
|---|---|
| Author's name in text | Magny develops this argument (67-69). |
| Author's name in reference (parenthetical) | This argument has been developed elsewhere (Magny 67-69). |
| Two authors' names in reference | The most notorious foreign lobby in Washington is the "Sugar Mafia" (Howe and Trott 134). |
| Quotation found in an indirect/"secondhand" source | The philosopher Alain states that "admiration is not pleasure but a kind of attention. . ." (qtd. in Magny 66). |

Note: MLA in-text citations use **author + page number only** — no year, no comma between them: `(Author page)`.

## Works Cited examples (verbatim from Pitt guide)

| Material Type | Works Cited |
|---|---|
| Book in print | Card, Claudia. *The Atrocity Paradigm: A Theory of Evil*. Oxford UP, 2005. |
| eBook | Gaither, Milton. *Homeschool: An American History*. Palgrave Macmillan, 2017. *SpringerLink*, doi-org.pitt.idm.oclc.org/10.1057/978-1-349-95056-0. |
| An article in a print journal | Doggart, Julia. "Minding the Gap: Realizing Our Ideal Community Writing Assistance Program." *The Community Literacy Journal*, vol. 2, no. 1, 2007, pp. 71-80. |
| An article in an electronic journal | Sherrard-Johnson, Cherene. "'A Plea for Color': Nella Larsen's Iconography of the Mulatta." *American Literature*, vol. 76, no. 4, 2004, pp. 833-869, doi:10.1215/00029831-76-4-833. |
| An encyclopedia entry | "Patanjali." *Benét's Reader's Encyclopedia*, edited by Bruce Murphy, 4th ed., HarperCollins Publishers, 1996, p. 782. |
| A government publication | United States, Federal Maritime Commission. *Hawaiian Trade Study: An Economic Analysis*. Government Printing Office, 1978. |
| An interview you conducted | Brandt, Deborah. Personal interview. 28 May 2008. (List under the interviewee's name.) |
| A film/DVD | Depends on the work's focus — see the MLA Style blog for a detailed explanation. Example (9th ed. clarification): *Blade Runner*. 1982. Directed by Ridley Scott, director's cut, Warner Bros., 1992. |
| A page on a website, no author | "Stunning Lakeside View on Lake Erie." *VisitPA*, Commonwealth of Pennsylvania, 7 June 2018, www.visitpa.com/article/stunning-lakeside-views-lake-erie. |
| A page on a website, with an author | Del Castillo, Inigo. "How Not to Kill Your Houseplants, According to Botanists." *Apartment Therapy*, 29 Jan. 2020, www.apartmenttherapy.com/houseplant-tips-botanists-36710191. |
| Artwork, from a website | Sherald, Amy. *Former First Lady, Michelle Obama*. 2018. *National Portrait Gallery*, npg.si.edu/object/npg_NPG.2018.15. |

Some instructors require access dates for websites — check with the instructor if unsure.

## Generative AI in MLA

MLA explicitly does **not** treat the AI tool as an "author." Instead:

| Element | What goes here |
|---|---|
| Author | (omit — MLA does not recommend treating the AI tool as author) |
| Title of Source | Describe what was generated (often the prompt, in quotation marks) |
| Title of Container | Name of the AI tool (e.g. *Claude*) |
| Version | The specific model/version (e.g. Sonnet 4.5) |
| Publisher | The company that made the tool (e.g. Anthropic) |
| Date | Date the content was generated |
| Location | Stable, shareable URL to the generated content |

Works-Cited example: `"Describe the theme of nature in Jane Austen's Mansfield Park" prompt. ChatGPT, model GPT-4o, OpenAI, 23 Sept. 2024, chatgpt.com/share/66f1b0a0-d704-8000-be9a-85f53c850607.`
In-text example: `("Describe the theme").`
