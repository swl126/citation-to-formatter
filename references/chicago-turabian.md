# Chicago 18th Edition & Turabian 9th Edition

Source: [Pitt Library — Chicago 18th/Turabian 9th](https://pitt.libguides.com/citationhelp/chicago) and [Turabian 9th](https://pitt.libguides.com/citationhelp/turabian)

Chicago style has been published by the University of Chicago Press since 1906. Turabian (Kate L. Turabian's *Manual for Writers of Research Papers, Theses, and Dissertations*, 9th ed.) is essentially the same system with slight simplifications aimed at student writers — use the Chicago examples and Turabian examples interchangeably as templates; if the user specifically says "Turabian," use the Turabian-labeled table for exact punctuation, otherwise default to Chicago.

Both present **two documentation systems** — pick based on discipline, or ask the user:

- **Notes-Bibliography style** — humanities (literature, history, arts). Footnote/endnote per citation + a bibliography.
- **Author-Date style** — physical/natural/social sciences. Parenthetical `(Author Year, page)` + a reference list.

## Chicago — Notes-Bibliography style

Note: number sequentially through the paper; the first note for a source is the **full note**, later notes to the same source are **shortened/duplicate notes** (author's last name + short title + page).

| Material Type | Note (full, then duplicate) | Bibliography |
|---|---|---|
| A book in print | 1. Michael Pollan, *The Omnivore's Dilemma: A Natural History of Four Meals* (New York: Penguin, 2006), 99–100. — Duplicate: 2. Pollan, *Omnivore's Dilemma*, 3. | Pollan, Michael. *The Omnivore's Dilemma: A Natural History of Four Meals*. New York: Penguin, 2006. |
| An article in a print journal | 1. Joshua I. Weinstein, "The Market in Plato's *Republic*," *Classical Philology* 104 (2009): 440. — Duplicate: 2. Weinstein, "Plato's *Republic*," 452–53. | Weinstein, Joshua I. "The Market in Plato's *Republic*." *Classical Philology* 104 (2009): 439–58. |

Note author format: `First Last` in the footnote/endnote itself, but `Last, First` in the bibliography entry.
